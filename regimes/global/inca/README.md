# Inca Empire / 印加帝国

> *By the light of Inti, Father Sun, Tahuantinsuyu endures.*

## Overview

The Inca Empire (1438–1533), known as **Tahuantinsuyu** (Land of the Four Quarters), was the largest empire in pre-Columbian America. Stretching 4,000 km along the Andes from modern Colombia to Chile, it governed 10–12 million people without money, markets, or a writing system — relying instead on labor tribute (mit'a), reciprocity, and the quipu recording system.

## Governance Model

**Pattern:** `centralized-hierarchy`
**System:** Sapa Inca + Tahuantinsuyu (萨帕·印卡四方制)

The Sapa Inca was the divine and absolute ruler — the Son of Inti (the Sun God). The empire was divided into four suyus (quarters), each governed by an Apu who reported directly to the Sapa Inca in Cusco. Administration was organized on a decimal system from groups of 10 households up to 10,000.

## Key Features

- **No money** — the economy runs entirely on mit'a labor tribute and reciprocity
- **Quipu** — knotted-string recording system for census, tribute, and history
- **Mit'a** — labor tax: every ayllu contributes labor for state projects
- **Ayllu** — communal kinship group as the basic social and economic unit
- **Qhapaq Nan** — 40,000 km royal road network with chasqui relay runners
- **Decimal administration** — population managed in groups of 10 to 10,000

## Historical Context

The Inca state expanded rapidly under **Pachacuti** (1438–1471), who transformed the Kingdom of Cusco into Tahuantinsuyu. His son **Tupac Inca Yupanqui** and grandson **Huayna Capac** extended the empire to its maximum extent. A civil war between **Atahualpa** and **Huascar** weakened the empire just before **Francisco Pizarro** arrived with 168 conquistadors in 1532. Atahualpa was captured at Cajamarca and executed in 1533, effectively ending independent Inca rule.
