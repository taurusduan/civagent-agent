# Mughal Empire / 莫卧儿帝国

> *Hukum-e-Padshahi — by the command of the Emperor, let it be so.*

## Overview

The Mughal Empire (1526–1857) was one of the largest and most prosperous empires in world history, ruling the Indian subcontinent at its zenith under emperors Akbar, Jahangir, Shah Jahan, and Aurangzeb. At its peak in the late 17th century, it commanded approximately 25% of global GDP and governed over 150 million subjects.

## Governance Model

**Pattern:** `centralized-hierarchy`
**System:** Mansabdar System (曼萨布达尔制)

The Mughal state was organized around the **mansabdar system** — a non-hereditary ranking system where every official held a dual rank:
- **Zat** (personal rank) — determined status and salary
- **Sawar** (cavalry rank) — determined military obligations

All ranks were granted and revoked solely by the Padshah, ensuring that loyalty flowed upward to the throne rather than to local dynasties.

## Key Features

- **Sulh-i-Kul** (Universal Peace) — religious tolerance as state policy under Akbar's tradition
- **Persian court culture** — Persian as the language of administration and literature
- **Indo-Islamic synthesis** — architecture, art, music, and cuisine blending Central Asian, Persian, and Indian traditions
- **Jagir system** — revenue assignments to officials in lieu of cash salary
- **Subah-sarkar-pargana** — three-tier provincial administration

## Historical Context

Founded by **Babur** (descendant of Timur and Genghis Khan) after the Battle of Panipat in 1526. Consolidated by **Akbar** (1556–1605), who created the mansabdar system and the policy of religious tolerance. Reached architectural splendor under **Shah Jahan** (Taj Mahal, Red Fort). Expanded to maximum extent under **Aurangzeb** (1658–1707), but his policies sowed the seeds of decline. The empire gradually fragmented in the 18th century and was formally abolished by the British in 1857.
